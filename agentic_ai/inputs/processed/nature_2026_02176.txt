[SECTION: Abstract]
Here we invented an assay called 'SpyTag-Catcher Immobilization of Lanmodulin for Assaying Metal-Binding Selectivity' (SpyCI-LAMBS) to measure metalloprotein selectivity en masse. This 96-format workflow was used to study the selectivity of 621 lanmodulin (LanM) orthologs for 15 REs, revealing eight distinct selectivity profiles based on sequence-to-function analyses. We discovered >200 LanMs with stronger selectivity against low-value LaIII relative to the prototypical LanM. This includes a LanM that can perform a challenging one-stage separation of PrIII from LaIII with up to >99.9 mol% purity and 83% yield.
---
[SECTION: Introduction]
The lanthanide-binding protein lanmodulin (LanM) from Methylobacterium extorquens (Mex-LanM) has emerged as a promising candidate for RE purification. Mex-LanM exhibits extraordinarily high selectivity for REs and sufficient intra-RE selectivity when immobilized in column format to achieve individual separation among some RE pairs (for example, $Nd^{III}$ versus $Dy^{III}$). However, Mex-LanM exhibits similar affinity for $Ce^{III}$ through $Eu^{III}$, which limits the separation potential across light REs (LRE; $La^{III}$–$Sm^{III}$). A recent effort identified Hans-LanM from Hansschlegelia quercus in a bioinformatic campaign that unearthed 696 putative LanM orthologs. Hans-LanM exhibits lanthanide-dependent dimerization and substantially enhanced selectivity for $La^{III}$ to $Nd^{III}$ compared to heavy REs (HREs; $Eu^{III}$–$Lu^{III}$, $Y^{III}$). When immobilized, Hans-LanM enabled single-stage separation of a 95:5 $Nd^{III}$/$Dy^{III}$ solution.

[SECTION: Results — LanM selectivity is consistent on-resin and in-solution]
In prior work, we found the relationship among apparent dissociation constants ($K_{D,app}$) determined in-solution and logD values determined on-resin to agree on the same overall selectivity trends for the reported Mex-LanM and Hans-LanM. This is important because it indicates that the observed selectivities on-resin are not immobilization artefacts. For example, Hans-LanM's logD values from SpyCI-LAMBS are consistent with the determined $K_{D,app}$ values for the R100K (monomeric) variant (a 27-fold weaker affinity for $Dy^{III}$ compared to $Nd^{III}$). To investigate whether this notion held true across the selectivity clusters, we purified representative proteins (o-412, C1; o-543, C2; o-585, C3; o-36 and o-127, C5) and studied their binding properties in-solution with several REs.

[SECTION: Results — LanM selectivity is consistent on-resin and in-solution]
Based on o-412's logD profile compared to Mex-LanM (Supplementary Fig. 17), we expected similar relative affinities among LREs and marginally weaker relative affinities for HREs (Supplementary Table 2). Using CD, we indeed found that o-412 had similar relative affinities for the LREs (1.6-fold difference between $Pr^{III} and $La^{III}), and weaker relative affinity for HREs (6.2-fold difference between $Pr^{III}$ and $Dy^{III}$) compared to 4.7-fold difference between the similarly distant (among the $Ln^{III}$ series) $Nd^{III}$ and $Ho^{III}$ for Mex-LanM. The C5 members o-36 and o-127 were of particular interest, due to their steeper selectivity against $La^{III}$ according to SpyCI-LAMBS. We used spectrofluorometry to allow direct comparison of these proteins. Both LanMs displayed notably weaker relative affinity for $La^{III}$ than that observed for Mex-LanM and for the monomeric version of Hans-LanM (R100K; Fig. 5b,c and Supplementary Table 2); o-36 had a striking 8.7-fold difference in affinity between $Nd^{III}$ and $La^{III}$, whereas this difference was 3.5-fold for o-127 and 1.7-fold for Mex-LanM.

[SECTION: Results — C5 LanMs reject lanthanum for enhanced RE separations]
From each cluster, we initially chose members with the highest Mtot values (Fig. 2c). We then picked the final five candidates based on their high soluble protein expression levels and unique separation factor (SF) heatmaps (Supplementary Figs. 29–34)—o-25 (C7) from Methylobacterium aerolatum, o-36 (C5) from Methylobacterium sp.13MFTsu3.1M2, o-40 (C5) from Methylobacterium sp.P1-11, o-257 (C1) from Bradyrhizobium japonicum and o-380 (C4) from Methylopila sp.73B (Extended Data Fig. 5 and Supplementary Fig. 35).

[SECTION: Results — C5 LanMs reject lanthanum for enhanced RE separations]
We then purified o-36 and o-621 with a -GSGC tag to fabricate 0.8-ml agarose columns with a confirmed 2 μmolRE ml−1 agarose capacity, determined by $Sm^{III}$ breakthrough (Supplementary Fig. 39). o-36's logD profile determined using the traditional column approach was nearly identical to the profile determined using SpyCI-LAMBS, especially in the $La^{III}$–$Nd^{III}$ region (Supplementary Fig. 38). Both columns were fed a binary 50:50 solution of $La^{III}$ and $Sm^{III}$ with bound REs eluted using a two-step pH gradient (Fig. 6c,d, Extended Data Fig. 7 and Supplementary Fig. 40). o-36 achieved 94.7 mol% La purity at 97.2% yield, and 99.2 mol% Sm purity at 88.8% yield, whereas o-621 could not achieve separation of these two REs under the same conditions. Motivated by this result, we tested the more challenging $Pr^{III}$/$La^{III}$ separation using a 50:50 binary solution of the two REs. With a single step-drop from pH 2.4 to 1.5, o-36 achieved >99.9 mol% $Pr^{III}$ with 83.2% yield. To confirm this ability to reject $La^{III}$ was reproducible, we refabricated new 0.4-ml agarose columns with a new batch of proteins. Without optimization of the elution timings and pH increments to accommodate the smaller scale, we still achieved a 98.6 ± 1.1 mol% $Sm^{III}$ purity with a 72.6 ± 1.1% yield, and 96.5 ± 0.2 mol% $Pr^{III}$ purity with a 72.0 ± 5.8% yield (Extended Data Fig. 8). In light of this demonstration, we name o-36 as 'Melba-LanM', a prototypical representative of the C5 selectivity cluster that reproducibly overcomes the LRE selectivity plateau of Mex-LanM and Hans-LanM.

[SECTION: Discussion]
While the LRE SFs of Melba-LanM are comparable with those for a recently discovered and further engineered protein, Mex-LanD–E75Q/E78A (SFPr/La of 5.1), which enriches for $Nd^{III}$/$Pr^{III}$ over $La^{III}$ through selective dimerization, the compatibility of Melba-LanM with a column-based process enables the propagation of its SFs over multiple theoretical plates, resulting in effective intra-LRE ion separation.

For example, C1 orthologs like o-412 (from Methyloligella sp. GL2) have selectivity maxima centered around $Nd^{III}$ and greater selectivity against $Dy^{III}$ than Mex-LanM. [SECTION: Methods — Strains and plasmid construction]
To prepare these vectors, we first obtained a pET-29b(+) plasmid from Twist Bioscience containing Mex-LanM (o-621) with an appended GSG spacer followed by a SpyTag (RGVPHIVMVDAYKRYK) immediately 5′ upstream from the stop codon.
---
[SECTION: Methods — Expression and purification of untagged LanMs (for in-solution characterization)]
o-543 (Ensifer adhaerens), o-585 (Rhizobium sp. BK275), o-412 (Methyloligella sp. GL2), o-127 (Methylosinus sp. sav-2) and o-36 (Methylobacterium sp. 13MFTsu3.1M2), with predicted signal peptides removed and codon-optimized for expression in E. coli, were synthesized and purchased from Twist Biosciences in pET-29b. These proteins were expressed as described for Mex-LanM with the only modification being that the IPTG induction time was 4 h. Purification of all proteins followed the procedure for Mex-LanM, except that the high-salt Q-Sepharose buffer (buffer M2) contained 600 mM NaCl instead of 1 M NaCl. Proteins were stored at −80 °C until further use. The yields of purified LanM proteins were 40 mg l−1 of culture (o-543), 70 mg l−1 of culture (o-585), 30 mg l−1 of culture (o-127), 40 mg l−1 of culture (o-412) and 20 mg l−1 of culture (o-36). Purification of o-21 (Methylobacterium sp. E-005) was also attempted, but its level of expression was low and was not obtained in sufficient purity for detailed characterization.

[SECTION: Methods — Determination of apparent dissociation constants using fluorescence spectroscopy]
The steady-state fluorescence emission of the tyrosine residue in o-36 was measured to determine the apparent dissociation constant ($K_{D,app}$). Emission spectra of the protein were measured with a Horiba Fluorolog-QM fluorometer, which featured a double monochromator for excitation and a single monochromator for emission. A quartz cuvette with dimensions of 10 × 2 mm (Starna Cells) was used. As previously described, small molecule chelators such as diethylenetriaminepentaacetic acid (DTPA) or ethylene glycol-bis(β-aminoethyl ether)-N,N,N',N'-tetraacetic acid (EGTA) were used to buffer the free metal ion concentration45. Briefly, the metal-chelator equivalence point was first determined using the XO competition assay. Once the equivalence point for each metal and chelator stock solution was determined, the low-chelator-buffered and high-chelator-buffered metal solutions were prepared. The low-buffered solution contained 20 mM acetate, 100 mM KCl and 10 mM chelator at pH 5.0, while the high-buffered solution contained 20 mM acetate, 100 mM KCl and 10 mM Ln-chelator at the same pH. DTPA was used for La, whereas EGTA was used for the other lanthanides. The protein was diluted to a final concentration of 2 µM in both the low and high metal-buffered solutions. These solutions were mixed in different ratios (500 µl total) to achieve a wide range of free metal concentrations. The prepared samples were incubated at room temperature for ~16 h to ensure equilibrium was reached. Steady-state emission scans were performed with excitation at 265 nm, using the excitation slit width of 5 nm/5 nm/5 nm (entrance slit/central slit/exit slit; hereafter, En/Ctr/Ex) and the emission slit width of 5 nm/5 nm (En/Ex), and an integration time of 0.5 s. The fluorescence intensity at 302 nm was plotted against the calculated free metal concentrations. The data were fitted to the Hill equation to determine the KD,app and the Hill coefficient (n).
